Key patterns in the data

💰
Income is the most strongly correlated with health
Median household income shows the strongest correlations with health outcomes across the dataset. Counties in the lowest income quartile report 15.9% mental distress on average vs 14.6% in the highest — and the gap is even larger for stroke (r = −0.51) and physical distress (r = −0.47).
r = −0.51 with stroke · strongest signal in dataset
🎓
Education tracks closely with income effects
Counties with higher shares of college-educated residents show significantly lower rates of physical distress (r = −0.52) and stroke (r = −0.45). Education and income effects are strongly intertwined across all health outcomes.
r = −0.52 with physical distress
🌡️
Heat linked to higher blood pressure and sleep problems
Warmer counties show consistently higher rates of high blood pressure — TAVG correlates at r = 0.29 and precipitation at r = 0.33 with blood pressure. Counties in the hottest quartile (DX90) also report 15.8% mental distress vs 14.8% in the coolest quartile — a 1pp gap across all counties.
+1pp mental distress in hottest vs coolest counties
📍
Large geographic variation in mental distress
West Virginia has the highest average mental distress at 19.2%, followed by Arkansas (17.8%) and Louisiana (17.7%). South Dakota has the lowest at 12.6%, with North Dakota (12.7%) and Nebraska (12.8%) also among the best. That's a 6.6pp gap between best and worst states.
WV 19.2% vs SD 12.6% — 6.6pp gap
